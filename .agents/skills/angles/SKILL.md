# Angles

You analyze and sharpen the angle of marketing copy. The angle is the emotional lens you frame an offer through. Same product, different angle, different result.

## Core idea

strong angle leads to targeted emotion leads to predictable action.

Predictable action means sharing, signing up, buying, or engaging. Pick the emotion you want first, then choose the angle that triggers it. A page with no clear angle informs but does not move anyone.

## The 6 psychology-backed angles

Each angle targets a specific emotional state. Use the formula to write the line, and swap the brackets for the reader's real situation.

### 1. Curiosity (information gap)
- Psychology: people seek to close the gap between what they know and what they want to know. Curiosity is an itch that demands scratching.
- Formula: "Most [people] don't know [surprising thing] exists. Here's what happens when you find out."
- Example: "Most marketers don't know their transformation copy stopped working at Stage 3. Here's what to do instead."

### 2. Relatability (self-referential processing)
- Psychology: when people see themselves in the content, the brain encodes it deeper and recalls it better. "This is about me" grabs attention.
- Formula: "Have you ever [specific relatable moment], and just [exact feeling]?"
- Example: "Have you ever spent 3 hours on a post that got 4 likes and wondered if any of this is working?"

### 3. Challenge (cognitive dissonance)
- Psychology: when new information conflicts with an existing belief, the brain feels discomfort and seeks resolution. That tension drives attention and memory.
- Formula: "You've been [doing common thing] wrong." Or "[thing everyone believes] is not what actually [does the thing]."
- Example: "You've been writing hooks wrong." Or "transformation copy is not what gets Stage 3 buyers to convert."

### 4. Loss aversion (prospect theory)
- Psychology: people feel losses about twice as intensely as equivalent gains. The pain of missing out drives faster decisions.
- Formula: "Every [time period] you [don't do thing], you are [losing specific thing]."
- Example: "Every week you write without a keyword plan, a competitor is taking the ranking you could have owned."

### 5. Fear plus efficacy (extended parallel process model)
- Psychology: fear only drives action when both the threat feels real AND the reader believes they can do something about it. Fear without a fix creates paralysis, not action.
- Formula: "If [threat], here is the exact [specific action] that fixes it in [timeframe]."
- Example: "If AI overviews are answering your buyers without citing you, here's the one content move that gets you back in frame."

### 6. Belonging (in-group and out-group)
- Psychology: people define themselves partly by group membership. When an action aligns with identity ("people like us do things like this"), motivation comes from belonging, not just personal benefit.
- Formula: "This is for [specific identity] who are done with [thing that conflicts with that identity]."
- Example: "Not for everyone. For operators who are done guessing what to write next."

## How to apply

1. Diagnose the reader. What emotional state are they in, and what would actually make them act? That decides the angle, not the other way around.
2. Score the existing copy. Map it to the 6 angles: which are present, which are missing, which are strong. A scorecard makes the gaps obvious.
3. Choose 1 or 2 dominant angles to lead. Do not cram all 6 onto one page. Stacking every angle dilutes each one. Let the rest play supporting roles.
4. Write each line with its formula, aimed at the one emotion it targets.
5. Place by position in the page or funnel:
   - Hook and top of page: curiosity, relatability, loss. Earn the next scroll.
   - Near the offer and proof: fear plus efficacy. The threat must sit right beside the fix, or it paralyzes instead of converting.
   - Near the decision or CTA: belonging. Identity tips a maybe into a yes right before they act.
6. Stay honest. Never fabricate a statistic, a result, or a threat to power an angle. A false angle converts once and then burns trust. If the proof is not real yet, use a weaker true angle or leave a placeholder.

## Output format

When analyzing a page, return:

- Angle scorecard: a short table of angle, where it shows up now, and strength (strong, partial, missing).
- Lead angles: the 1 or 2 you would build the page around, and why they fit this reader.
- Recommendations: for each, the angle, the emotion it targets, an example line in the formula's voice, and exactly where it goes.
- What not to touch: angles already doing the work, so they do not get diluted.
